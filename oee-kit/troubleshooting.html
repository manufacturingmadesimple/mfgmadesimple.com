Day 1: Baseline Assessment

Problem 1: "I don't have good data"

Symptoms / Root Cause

No downtime logs, no production count records, no quality tracking. Everything is "word of mouth". Root Cause: Most small manufacturers don't track OEE systematically. You're not alone.

Option A: Start Fresh (Recommended)
  1. Pick ONE shift on ONE line
  2. Print the Daily Data Collection Sheet
  3. Assign someone to shadow the line for 8 hours
  4. Record EVERYTHING that happens: Start time, break times, every stop (even 2 minutes), pieces produced each hour, any defects/rework.
Option B: Estimate from Available Data
  1. Use production schedule for total time
  2. Ask supervisor: "How much downtime did we have yesterday?"
  3. Check shipping records for pieces produced
  4. Ask quality: "How many rejects/rework yesterday?"
  5. Start with educated guesses, improve accuracy next week
Option C: Use Industry Averages as Baseline

Average manufacturing OEE: 60%. Typical availability: 85%. Typical performance: 85%. Typical quality: 95%. Use these, then measure actual next week.

💡 Pro Tip: Imperfect data beats no data. Start with what you have, improve over time.

Problem 2: "My OEE calculation seems wrong"

Symptoms

Numbers don't match gut feel. OEE dramatically different from expectations. Can't figure out where math went wrong.

Root Cause: The most common OEE calculation errors:

❌ ERROR #1: Not subtracting Planned Downtime first

Availability = (Total Time - All Downtime) ÷ Total Time
Example: (480 - 75) ÷ 480 = 84.4%

✅ CORRECT

Run Time = Total Time - Planned Downtime (Ex: 480 - 30 = 450 min)
Avail. = (Run Time - Unplanned Downtime) ÷ Run Time
Example: (450 - 45) ÷ 450 = 90%

❌ ERROR #2: Using actual cycle time instead of ideal

Performance = Total Pieces ÷ Operating Time
Example: 750 ÷ 405 = 1.85 pieces/min

✅ CORRECT

Performance = (Total Pieces × Ideal Cycle Time) ÷ Operating Time
Example: (750 × 0.5) ÷ 405 = 92.6%

❌ ERROR #3: Forgetting to include rework

Defects = Scrap only
Example: 15 pieces scrapped = 15 defects

✅ CORRECT

Defects = Scrap + Rework
Example: 15 scrapped + 10 reworked = 25 defects

Validation Check:
  • Availability should be 70-95% (below 70% = serious downtime issues)
  • Performance should be 60-95% (above 100% = wrong ideal cycle time)
  • Quality should be 90-99% (below 90% = major quality problems)
  • OEE should be 40-85% (world-class = 85%+)
If your numbers are outside these ranges, double-check your math.

Problem 3: "The numbers don't make sense"

Diagnostic Questions
  • Is your ideal cycle time actually ideal? Time the machine running at absolute max speed. Not "average" speed.
  • Did you measure a typical day? Don't measure the "best" or "worst" day to skew data. Pick a random Tuesday/Wednesday.
  • Are you double-counting downtime? Planned downtime should NOT include unplanned stops. Don't count the same minutes twice.
  • Did you count ALL the pieces? Include good + bad pieces in "total produced". Not just shipped pieces.

Solution: Walk through the calculation step-by-step with someone else. Fresh eyes catch errors.

Problem 4: "I'm getting OEE over 100%"

Diagnosis: This is mathematically impossible. You have an input error.

  • Cause 1: Ideal Cycle Time is too high. You entered 5.0 instead of 0.5 minutes per piece. Check your decimal point.
  • Cause 2: Operating Time is wrong. Operating time can't be more than Total Scheduled Time. Check: Total Time → subtract Planned → subtract Unplanned = Operating.
  • Cause 3: Confusing minutes with hours. If cycle time is in minutes, all times must be in minutes.

Problem 5: "My baseline is shockingly low (under 50%)"

First: Don't panic. This is actually GOOD NEWS. Here's why:

The Math:
Every OEE point = $5,000-$15,000 annually (typical). At 45% OEE, you have 40 points of opportunity to reach 85% (world-class).
40 points × $10,000 average = $400,000 annual opportunity.

Reality Check: Industry average OEE = 60%. 45-55% is common for unoptimized operations. You're not broken, you're just unoptimized. Your improvement potential is HUGE. Small changes = big ROI.

What to do: Verify the math, accept the reality, and be excited about the opportunity. Fix one thing and watch it improve.

Day 2: Quick Win Identification

Problem 6: "I can't find any quick wins"

Symptoms

Everything seems to require major investment. All problems look complex. Can't see low-hanging fruit.

Solution: Change Your Lens. You're looking for the wrong things. Quick wins hide in:

  • Look for WAITING: Operator waiting for material, machine waiting for operator. Quick Win: Relocate, reorganize, or pre-stage.
  • Look for SEARCHING: Looking for tools, paperwork, or parts. Quick Win: Labels, shadow boards, standard locations.
  • Look for VARIATION: Different operators different speeds, quality varies by shift. Quick Win: Standardize the best method.
  • Look for RECURRING: Same jam happens daily, same setup step causes delays. Quick Win: Fix the root cause once.
💡 Quick Win Checklist:
☐ Can we eliminate unnecessary movement?
☐ Can we eliminate waiting for X?
☐ Can we prevent this error from happening?
☐ Can we make the right way the easy way?
☐ Can we reduce variation?

Still stuck? Walk the line for 2 hours. Count how many times you see someone walking to get something, someone asking a question, or a machine sitting idle but not broken.

Problem 7: "Everything requires capital approval"

Reframe: Most quick wins cost $0-$100. You're thinking too big.

$0 Quick Wins:

  • Reorganize workspace (5S)
  • Document standard work
  • Cross-train operators
  • Move material staging location
  • Change sequence of operations
  • Better communication (shift handoff)

<$100 Quick Wins:

  • Shadow boards (pegboard + hooks)
  • Go/no-go gauges
  • Simple fixtures/jigs
  • Color-coding materials
  • Visual management boards
  • Timer for tracking

Challenge yourself: Find 3 improvements that cost nothing but reorganization.

Problem 8: "I have too many opportunities"

Solution: Force Rank using the Impact/Effort Matrix.

  1. Score Impact (1-5): 1 = Saves 1-2% OEE | 3 = Saves 3-5% OEE | 5 = Saves 6%+ OEE
  2. Score Effort (1-5): 1 = Can do today | 3 = Can do this week | 5 = Requires approval/major time
  3. Calculate Priority: Priority = Impact × (6 - Effort)
    • High impact (5), Low effort (1): 5 × 5 = 25 ⭐⭐⭐
    • High impact (5), High effort (5): 5 × 1 = 5
    • Low impact (2), Low effort (1): 2 × 5 = 10

Rule: Only work on 3 improvements at once. Finish before starting more.

Problem 9: "Management won't support improvements"

Diagnosis: Management isn't against improvement. They're against risk of making things worse, disruption to production, and unproven ideas.

Solution: Speak Their Language

Instead of: "We should reorganize the changeover cart"
Say: "Changeovers take 45 mins. Industry standard is 20. That's costing us $X,XXX annually. I can test a reorganization this Saturday during downtime. Zero risk to production. Can I try it?"

💡 Magic Words: "Can I test...", "Zero risk to production...", "I'll do it on my own time...". Don't ask for permission to improve. Ask for permission to TEST.

Day 3: Action Planning

Problem 10: "No one will take ownership"

Root Cause: People don't resist improvement. They resist more work added to their plate, blame if it fails, and unclear expectations.

Solution: Make It Easy to Say Yes.
❌ BAD Request: "Can you lead the changeover improvement project?"
✅ GOOD Request: "Can you time the next 3 changeovers and tell me the average? Just write start/end time on this sheet. I'll do the rest."

Ownership Escalation:
Week 1: "Can you collect data?" → Week 2: "Can you test this idea?" → Month 2: "Can you lead the next improvement?"

Pro Tip: The person who complains the most often cares the most. Recruit them.

Problem 11: "I can't get the resources I need"

Creative Resource Acquisition:

  • For Tools/Materials: Check maintenance for spare parts, borrow from another line temporarily, make it yourself (cardboard prototype), ask "what do we already have that could work?".
  • For Time: Use natural downtime (changeovers, weekends), break big projects into 15-minute chunks, do prep work yourself, overlap with scheduled maintenance.
  • For People: Trade favors, involve people in designing the solution (they'll help implement), make it so easy anyone could do it.

Mindset Shift: Constraints breed creativity. Work with what you have.

Problem 12: "Operators are resistant to change"

Why this happens: They weren't asked for input, change is imposed from above, they don't understand why, or previous "improvements" made their job harder.

Solution: Involve Them Early
❌ DON'T: "We're implementing this new procedure starting Monday."
✅ DO: "I noticed we waste 10 minutes every changeover looking for tools. What if we tried organizing them on a shadow board? Would that help you?"

Magic Question: "What makes your job harder than it should be?" Then fix that. They'll trust you for the next improvement.

Problem 13: "We don't have time to implement"

Reality Check: If you're always too busy to improve, you'll always be too busy.

Solution: Micro-Implementation. Instead of reorganizing the entire workstation, move just the tools for Product A during the next changeover (5 minutes). Instead of implementing new standard work for all operators, write down what the fastest operator does and try it tomorrow.

💡 Time Investment by Quick Win Type:
• Reorganization: 15-30 minutes
• Standard work documentation: 1 hour
• Shadow board installation: 2 hours
• Training operators: 15 minutes each
Do improvements during lunch breaks, shift changeovers, or scheduled downtime.

Day 4: Execute & Track

Problem 14: "Improvements aren't working"

Diagnostic Steps:

  1. Give it time: Most improvements take 2-3 days to show results due to the learning curve and kinks being worked out. Wait 3-5 days before declaring failure.
  2. Did you actually implement it? Is the shadow board being used? Are operators following the new procedure? If not, it's an enforcement problem, not an idea problem.
  3. Measure the right thing: A changeover improvement won't show in quality. Track the specific metric you targeted.
  4. Was the root cause correct? Maybe you fixed a symptom. Example: You organized tools, but the real issue was waiting for quality to inspect the first piece. Talk to operators: "We tried X. Why didn't it work?"

Problem 15: "My OEE got worse after changes"

Why This Happens:
  • Learning Curve: New procedure = slower initially. Timeline: 2-5 days to get better than baseline.
  • Measuring the Wrong Day: Sample size of 1 day is unreliable. Track for a full week.
  • Unintended Consequences: Fixed one problem, created another (e.g. faster changeover but more quality issues).
  • The Change Wasn't the Problem: Correlation ≠ causation. Something else caused the dip.

What to do: If it's been < 3 days, wait it out. If > 3 days, talk to operators, adjust the implementation, and consider reverting if truly worse after 1 week.

Problem 16: "Team isn't following new procedures"

Root Causes & Fixes:

  • They don't know: Training was insufficient. Fix: Better training, clearer docs.
  • The new way is harder: Looks good on paper but doesn't work in reality. Fix: Revise with their input.
  • No accountability: No consequences for non-compliance. Fix: Daily verification for first 2 weeks.
  • Leadership doesn't support it: Supervisor undermines it. Fix: Get supervisor buy-in first.

Problem 17: "I can't track daily progress"

Simplify: You don't need to measure everything every day. Pick ONE metric (e.g., Downtime minutes per shift, or Defect count).

5-Minute Daily Tracking Example:
Monday: 38 min downtime (breakdown at 2pm)
Tuesday: 22 min downtime (much better!)
Wednesday: 31 min downtime (material delay)
Thursday: 18 min downtime (new record!)
Friday: 25 min downtime (normal)
This is enough to see trends. Do full OEE calculation weekly.

Day 5: Results & ROI

Problem 18: "I'm not seeing any improvement"

First: Verify Measurement. Did you measure the same way both times (same shift, operator, product)? Did you actually implement changes (not just planned)? Were the changes big enough?

If genuinely implemented with zero change, likely issues:

  • Wrong root causes: You fixed symptoms, not causes. Fix: Deeper problem analysis.
  • Too small sample size: 1-2 days of data is just noise. Fix: Track for a full week.
  • Other problems got worse: Look at individual factors (Availability, Performance, Quality) not just overall OEE.

What to do: Extend to Day 10, implement bigger improvements, or consult with an experienced person. First attempts aren't always successful. Learning what doesn't work = progress.

Problem 19: "How do I prove this to management?"

Build Your Case:

Level 1: Basic Proof

Before OEE: 64%
After OEE: 69%
Gain: 5 points
Evidence: Daily data sheets, Before/after photos.

Level 2: Financial Proof

5-point gain = 5% more output
Annual value: $2M
5% = $100,000 capacity
Cost: $87 → ROI: 1,149X

Level 3: Sustainable Proof: Show a 4-week trend proving the improvement is sustained and growing. Present a clean summary of what was changed, the results by factor (A,P,Q), the financial impact, and your proposed next steps. Show, don't just tell.

Problem 20: "What do I do next?"

  • Path 1: Sustain & Monitor: Continue weekly tracking, address backsliding immediately. (Best if you've hit 75%+ OEE)
  • Path 2: Go Deeper on Same Line: SMED for changeovers, TPM for reliability, root cause analysis. (Best if this line is your bottleneck)
  • Path 3: Scale to Other Lines: Apply 5-Day Kit to Line 2, train supervisors, build continuous improvement culture. (Best if you have multiple lines)

Recommendation: Months 1-2: Sustain gains on Line 1, apply to Line 2. Months 3-4: Sustain both, apply to Line 3. Months 5-6: Go deeper on biggest opportunity. Year 2: Tackle capital improvements with data to justify.

General Challenges

Problem 21: "I'm doing this alone with no support"

Reality: Most successful continuous improvement starts with one person who cares. Use the Lone Wolf Strategy:

  1. Phase 1: Prove It Works (Week 1-2). Pick the smallest improvement, do it yourself off-hours, document before/after, calculate ROI.
  2. Phase 2: Show Results (Week 3). Present to immediate supervisor with data, not opinions. Ask to try it on another area.
  3. Phase 3: Recruit Allies (Week 4-6). Find one operator who gets it and one supervisor who supports it.
  4. Phase 4: Make It Official (Month 2-3). Request 30 min in a staff meeting to present results and propose making it systematic.
💡 Pro Tip: Start smaller than you think. Document everything. Focus on wins that need no permission. Apple started in a garage; you're doing the same for CI at your company.

Problem 22: "Our production is too variable"

Reality Check: High variability means HUGE opportunity for improvement.

  • Product Mix Variability: Track OEE by product family, not by day.
  • Demand Fluctuations: Track OEE only on "normal" days, exclude outliers.
  • Equipment Reliability / Quality Issues: This is the problem to fix (TPM / Root Cause Analysis).

Modified Approach: Instead of measuring one day, measure 5 consecutive days and take the average. Variability itself is a problem worth solving.

Problem 23: "We run too many different products"

Solution: Simplify.

  • Option 1: Track by Product Family. Group products by cycle time (e.g., Small, Medium, Large). Calculate OEE separately.
  • Option 2: Use Weighted Average. (Total Ideal Minutes for all products) ÷ (Total Pieces) = Weighted Ideal Cycle Time.
  • Option 3: Track One Product. Pick your highest-volume product. Track OEE only when running that product. Improvements will transfer to other products.

Problem 24: "We're a job shop, not repetitive mfg"

Myth Buster: OEE absolutely applies to job shops. You just measure differently.

Job Shop OEE Formula:
Availability = (Time machines ran ÷ Time scheduled)
Performance = (Quoted hours ÷ Actual hours)
Quality = (Jobs right first time ÷ Total jobs)

Job shops benefit MORE from OEE improvement because labor is your biggest cost, and fast turnaround is your competitive advantage. Focus on reducing setup time (SMED) and improving quoting accuracy.

Summary & Checklists

When To Get Outside Help

After 2 weeks:

  • OEE calculation consistently wrong
  • Zero buy-in from anyone
  • Improvements making things worse
After 1 to 3 months:
  • Implemented 5+ improvements with no results
  • Hit plateau (can't get past 75% OEE)
  • Need capital investment justification
  • Need systematic approach for entire facility

Final Troubleshooting Checklist

Before declaring the 5-Day Kit "not working," verify:

  • I measured baseline accurately
  • I calculated OEE correctly
  • I identified at least 3 improvement opportunities
  • I actually implemented changes (not just planned)
  • I gave improvements 3-5 days to show results
  • I involved operators in the process
  • I tracked progress for a full week
  • I measured results the same way as baseline
